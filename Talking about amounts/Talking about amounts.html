Unit: Talking About Amounts 📊

1. Countable vs. Uncountable Nouns

Before choosing an "amount" word, you must check whether your noun can be counted with numbers or not:

Noun Type Definition Examples
Countable Things you can count using numbers. They can be singular or plural. books, friends, apples, folders
Uncountable Things you cannot count with numbers. They are always singular. water, homework, information, time

2. Large Quantities (A Lot)

  • A lot of / Lots of: Works with both countable and uncountable nouns. Perfect for everyday chat!
  • Many: Only used with countable nouns (mostly in negatives/questions).
  • Much: Only used with uncountable nouns (mostly in negatives/questions).
• I have a lot of files on GitHub. (Countable)
• This project takes a lot of time. (Uncountable)
• I don't have much homework tonight. (Uncountable)

3. Small Quantities (A Little / A Few)

  • A few: Used only with countable nouns. It means a small number (around 2 to 4 things).
  • A little: Used only with uncountable nouns. It means a small amount or portion.
• I wrote a few grammar rules today.
• I just need a little help with my code.

4. Watch Out for Common Mistakes! 🛑

Be careful not to mix up your quantities when writing about schoolwork:

Incorrect: I have many homeworks to do.
Correct: I have a lot of homework to do.
*(Homework is uncountable and never takes an "s")*
Incorrect: There are a little people on the website.
Correct: There are a few people on the website.
*(People can be counted!)*
